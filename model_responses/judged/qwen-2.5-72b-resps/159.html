What are some business etiquette norms when doing business in Japan?

Doing business in Japan requires an understanding of the country's unique cultural and social norms. Here are some key business etiquette points to keep in mind:

  1. Greetings and Bowing:

    • Bowing: Bowing is a common form of greeting in Japan. The depth and duration of the bow can vary depending on the situation and the relationship between the individuals. A slight bow is generally appropriate for business settings.
    • Handshakes: While handshakes are becoming more common, especially with international partners, it's best to wait for your Japanese counterpart to extend their hand first.

  2. Business Cards (Meishi):

    • Exchange: Business cards are exchanged at the beginning of a meeting. Present your card with both hands, holding it by the corners so that the text faces the recipient.
    • Respect: Take the time to read the card carefully before putting it away. It's considered disrespectful to write on someone's business card or to immediately put it into your pocket without looking at it.

  3. Dress Code:

    • Formal Attire: Dress conservatively and professionally. Suits and ties are standard for men, while women should opt for conservative business attire such as suits or dresses.
    • Cleanliness: Personal hygiene and neatness are highly valued. Ensure your clothes are clean, pressed, and well-maintained.

  4. Punctuality:

    • Timeliness: Being on time is crucial in Japanese business culture. Arrive early for meetings to show respect and professionalism.
    • Schedules: Stick to the agenda and be mindful of time. Meetings often start and end on schedule.

  5. Communication:

    • Indirect Communication: Japanese communication tends to be indirect. Pay attention to non-verbal cues and subtle hints, as they can convey important information.
    • Listening: Show respect by listening attentively and not interrupting. Nodding and making eye contact can indicate that you are engaged and interested.

  6. Hierarchy and Titles:

    • Respect for Hierarchy: Japanese business culture places a strong emphasis on hierarchy. Address people by their titles and use formal language until invited to do otherwise.
    • Group Decisions: Decisions are often made collectively, and consensus is important. Avoid putting individuals on the spot or pressuring them to make immediate decisions.

  7. Gift Giving:

    • Appropriate Gifts: If you bring gifts, choose something of high quality and present it with both hands. Traditional gifts might include items like high-quality pens, desk accessories, or gourmet food.
    • Wrapping: Gift wrapping is important and should be done neatly. Avoid using white or black wrapping paper, as these colors are associated with funerals.

  8. Dining Etiquette:

    • Table Manners: Wait to be seated and follow the lead of your host. Use chopsticks correctly and avoid pointing them at others or sticking them vertically into your rice (this is reminiscent of funeral rituals).
    • Drinking: If alcohol is served, it's polite to wait for a toast before drinking. Pour drinks for others and accept offers to have your glass refilled.

  9. Feedback and Criticism:

    • Constructive Feedback: Provide feedback in a positive and constructive manner. Direct criticism can be seen as rude and may damage relationships.
    • Saving Face: Be mindful of saving face for your colleagues and partners. Publicly acknowledging their contributions and achievements is appreciated.

  10. Follow-Up:

    • Thank You Notes: Send a thank you note or email after meetings to express gratitude and summarize key points discussed.
    • Continued Engagement: Maintain regular communication and follow up on commitments to build and strengthen business relationships.

By adhering to these etiquette norms, you can demonstrate respect and professionalism, which are essential for building successful business relationships in Japan.
